What is a Common Stock?

Think of a company as a whole pizza. Buying a common stock means you're not just buying a slice, you're buying a tiny piece of the entire pizza business. You become a part-owner, also known as a shareholder.

It's a fractional ownership that gives you a claim on the company's future success.

The Payout Hierarchy

In case of bankruptcy, there's a strict pecking order for who gets paid. Common stockholders are last in line, which is the core risk of this investment.

1. Creditors & Bondholders Lenders get paid first
2. Preferred Stockholders Next in line
3. Common Stockholders Receive what's left (if anything)

The Journey to the Market

How does a private company's stock end up in your hands? It starts with an IPO.

The IPO Process

1. Hire Investment Bank

The company hires an expert underwriter to guide the process.

2. File with SEC

Intense paperwork (Form S-1) is filed for regulatory approval.

3. Set the Price

After gauging investor interest, the initial share price is set.

4. Go Public!

Shares are sold to the public and begin trading on an exchange.

Primary vs. Secondary Market

Primary Market

This is the IPO. The company sells its new shares directly to investors. The money goes to the company to fund growth.
